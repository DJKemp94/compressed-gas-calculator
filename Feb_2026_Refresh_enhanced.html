What does this calculator do?

This calculator estimates the concentration of a gas released into a room or the remaining air composition after a hazardous release from compressed gas cylinders, liquefied gas containers, or cryogenic vessels. It also models the localised concentration within a Personal Bubble (12 m³) around the leak source, providing a proximity-risk assessment for anyone working near the gas.

It supports six input scenarios:

  • Known Volume: You already know the volume of gas (m³) and want to calculate the resulting concentration.
  • Compressed Gas: Calculates the gas volume from cylinder pressure and water capacity, then determines the concentration.
  • Liquefied Gas: Calculates the gas volume from the weight of the contents and the specific volume, then determines the concentration.
  • Cryogenic Vessel: Calculates gas volume from the liquid volume using expansion ratios for Liquid Nitrogen or Liquid Helium.
  • Dry Ice (CO₂): Calculates CO₂ gas volume from a provided dry ice mass.
  • Slow Leak: Estimates the steady-state concentration from a continuous gas leak in a ventilated room, alongside a Time to Hazard calculation that shows how long it would take for conditions to become dangerous if ventilation fails.

For every calculation, the calculator also provides a Personal Bubble Assessment ‐ a secondary result modelling a fixed 2×2×3 m zone around the leak source. This estimates the concentration a person would experience if standing directly at the release point.

⚠ Disclaimer: Assessing exposure to gas is a highly complex field involving factors such as gas behaviour, room geometry, ventilation dynamics, temperature, and human physiology. This calculator provides estimates for informational purposes only and must not be treated as a substitute for a professional risk assessment. If there is any doubt about the risk to people, specialist advice must always be sought from a competent person.

Results often represent a worst-case scenario (total release, perfect mixing). The University of Nottingham and its Health & Safety Department do not accept liability for decisions made solely on the basis of this calculator's output.

Advanced Leak-Rate Estimator

×

This tool provides a rough estimate of leak rate for the Slow Leak calculation. It is intended for screening and must not be treated as a substitute for equipment data, measured leakage, or a competent risk assessment.

Which calculation should I use? Use Gas Line (Post-Regulator) when the line contains gas, for example nitrogen that has already been vaporised and pressure-reduced. Use Liquid Nitrogen Line only when the line is carrying liquid nitrogen before it flashes to gas at the leak point.

Gas Line (Post-Regulator)

What is Upstream Regulator Max Flow? This is the maximum flow the regulator can physically supply. It is sometimes shown on the datasheet as maximum flow, capacity, SCFM, NL/min, or Cv. If you enter a value here, the estimator will cap the leak rate at that figure. If you are unsure, leave it blank and the estimator will use the orifice-based value only.

Illustrative examples only: a small point-of-use regulator may be of the order of 1 to 5 m³/hr, while higher-capacity line regulators can be substantially above this. Always use the equipment's actual rating where available.

Use only for gas leaks to atmosphere where the gas is at room temperature and the release is from a short opening such as a torn hose, failed fitting, or snapped small connection.

Liquid Nitrogen Line

When should this be used? Use this only where the pipework is carrying liquid nitrogen. If nitrogen is supplied to the equipment as a gas, or the line is downstream of a vaporiser and functioning as a gas line, use the Gas Line (Post-Regulator) calculation instead.

Use only for liquid nitrogen line leaks where you need a rough gaseous release rate for Oxygen Deficiency Hazard (ODH) screening. The result is the equivalent nitrogen gas hazard in m³/hr, not the liquid flow rate.

Do not use this calculation for gaseous nitrogen supplied by cylinder manifolds, on-site gas generators, or vaporised bulk systems where the relevant leak is already on the gas side.

Detailed Instructions & Reference Tables

⚠ Important: This calculator provides estimates for informational purposes only. It must not be used on its own to make emergency occupancy, re-entry, rescue, evacuation, or detector-placement decisions. Assessing exposure to gas is a highly complex field ‐ real-world conditions including gas stratification, turbulent mixing, room geometry, temperature, and ventilation dynamics can differ significantly from the idealised models used here. This tool must not be used as a replacement for a full risk assessment. If there is any doubt about the risk to people, specialist advice must always be sought from a competent person, or your institution's health and safety department.

How to use:

  1. Enter the Area/Gas Details (building, room, gas name) ‐ these appear on the printed report.
  2. Select the calculation type that matches your situation, and then provide all requested details.
  3. Where exact release conditions are unknown, use the worst-case credible release for the equipment and task, not normal operation or a best-case assumption.
  4. Click Calculate, and this will populate the fields at the bottom of the form automatically.
  5. Move down to the bottom of the form, enter the Room Volume, select the Gas Hazard Type, and click Calculate Concentration.
  6. Review both the Room Concentration result and the Personal Bubble Assessment (which models the concentration within a 2×2×3 m zone around the leak source).
  7. For Slow Leak calculations, also review the Worst-Case Time to Hazard ‐ this shows how quickly conditions could become dangerous if ventilation fails.
  8. Click Generate Report for a print-friendly summary including all results and recommendations.

What you need to know:

Calculation Type Required Information
Compressed Gas Pressure (bar), water capacity of cylinder (as it suggests, this the the volume of water the cylinder is capable of holding) (Litres)
Liquefied Gas Weight of cylinder contents (NOT including cylinder itself) (kg), specific volume* (m³/kg)
Cryogenic Vessel Liquid volume (Litres), gas type (LN₂ or LHe)
Dry Ice (CO₂) Dry ice mass (kg)
Slow Leak Leak rate (m³/hr), air changes per hour (ACH)

*Specific volume is the volume per kg of substance. See Engineering Toolbox.

Understanding results:

  • Room Concentration: The overall gas concentration (or remaining O₂ level) assuming the gas mixes perfectly throughout the room.
  • Personal Bubble: The localised concentration within a fixed 12 m³ zone (2×2×3 m) around the leak source. This is always more severe than the room average and indicates the risk to a person standing near the gas.
  • Time to Hazard (slow leak only): How long it would take for conditions to reach hazardous thresholds if all ventilation fails (0 ACH worst-case).
Interpret results with care: The room result is an average concentration only. Local concentrations near the source, floor, ceiling, pits, ducts, service voids, or poorly ventilated corners may be significantly worse. This calculator is a screening tool to support risk assessment, not a design calculation or a substitute for competent judgement.
Control hierarchy: Where significant risk remains, prioritise elimination, substitution, segregation, and ventilation before relying on fixed or personal gas detection. For toxic and flammable gases, always compare results with the gas-specific exposure limits or LEL and consider acute local exposure near the source, not only the room average.

Key thresholds:

  • Asphyxiant gases: Shows the resulting O₂ level. Below 19.5% = risk; below 18% = dangerous.
  • Toxic gases: Result in ppm. Compare to the WEL in EH40.
  • Flammable gases: Result as %. Detection required if >25% of the LEL for the gas.
  • Oxygen/CO₂ enrichment: Detection required above 23.5% for O₂ or above 0.5% for CO₂.
